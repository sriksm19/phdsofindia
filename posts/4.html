“I hadn’t stepped out of my home town for over 21 years. When I got accepted to one of the best institutes of the country for a master’s and had to leave home, I was extremely scared, scared that I suddenly had to manage everything on my own. I wondered how I was going to survive, whether I would be accepted among the brilliant minds there.

On the day of admission I saw all these smart, confident, sophisticated people around me and it intimidated me even further. I come from a lower middle class family and I felt like I didn’t belong, that I wouldn’t be able to fit in. There were people from all over the country: different states and cultures. I even remember telling my mother I didn’t want to study there!

But I did get admitted and eventually with time and a great set of friendly batchmates, I adapted to the new environment. I learnt to be more confident.

I have always loved Biology. Initially, due to lack of exposure and opportunities in school, I believed medical sciences was the only profession I could pursue if I wanted to study the subject. When that didn’t work out and I enrolled myself for a Bachelor’s in the same, I realized the diversity and potential of this field. It was a whole new, fascinating world for me.

Personally as well as academically I had a great time throughout the journey, until my Masters’ supervisor asked me to convert into a PhD student in the same laboratory. I rejected that proposal and he didn’t take it well. When I was leaving the institute and went to bid him adieu, instead of encouraging me for the future, he retorted, “If you ever get through the PhD program in this institute, it will be because of me. Trust me, it will be really difficult out there for you.”

His words stung bad but I wasn’t deterred. I believe in earning my opportunities rather than taking someone’s discouragement as my fate. Unfortunately at various times it has been other researchers who have discouraged me more than anyone else. At that point of time, I felt like someone had challenged my very interest in research although he himself was an admirer of my work.

I worked day and night on my PhD applications to various Indian institutes and eventually found myself working with one of the finest minds in my field. She is also currently the editor of some of the top scientific journals in the world.

Last year, I bumped into my Masters’ supervisor at a conference. He was shocked to know that I was a student of such a renowned scientist, someone he himself looks up to. The look on his face was priceless. And at that moment I knew, the only one who can limit me, my potential, my confidence, is me! Not my Masters’ supervisor nor anyone else.

I no more doubt myself. I know I am capable enough to contribute something significant to my field and I am determined to do it.”
